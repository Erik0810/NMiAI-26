Application

I am writing to apply for access to Google's Willow program in connection with my participation in the Norwegian AI Championships (NM i AI). I am currently a master's student at the University of Oslo's Department of Informatics, specializing in Programming and Systems Architecture.

My thesis work focuses on Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) solvers applied to optimization problems within the Norwegian railway infrastructure — specifically in collaboration with Bane NOR, the state enterprise responsible for the national rail network. This work has given me a solid background in large-scale computational problem solving and a genuine interest in leveraging modern hardware accelerators for computationally intensive tasks.

As part of NM i AI, I am competing in a grocery object detection challenge that requires training high-capacity deep learning models on a custom annotated retail dataset. I am currently working with YOLOv8x — one of the largest variants of the YOLO detection architecture — at an input resolution of 1536×1536 pixels. The dataset consists of approximately 250 images with over 22,000 bounding box annotations spanning 356 product categories. Training a model of this scale to convergence is highly GPU-intensive and requires significant VRAM (the YOLOv8x model alone occupies close to 20 GB during training at this resolution with a batch size of 2).

I have already been granted a GCP account with unlimited compute credits for the duration of this competition, and I am currently using a g2-standard-16 instance equipped with an NVIDIA L4 GPU (24 GB VRAM) in the europe-west1-c zone. While this setup has allowed me to train competitive models — achieving a leaderboard score of 0.9095 out of a maximum of approximately 0.93 for the current top entry — access to Willow's next-generation quantum-inspired or accelerated compute resources would allow me to explore more advanced training strategies that are currently bottlenecked by hardware.

Specifically, I am looking to:

1. Train a k-fold ensemble (3 folds) of YOLOv8x models simultaneously rather than sequentially, reducing total training time from approximately 15 hours to 5 hours.
2. Experiment with larger batch sizes and higher input resolutions (up to 2048 px) that are currently infeasible on a single L4 GPU.
3. Run extended hyperparameter search over augmentation strategies, confidence thresholds, and Weighted Boxes Fusion parameters that are too expensive to sweep on current hardware.

Beyond the competition, I believe exposure to Willow's capabilities would directly inform my thesis research, where I am exploring hybrid classical-quantum approaches to MILP relaxations for Bane NOR's train scheduling and track allocation problems. These are NP-hard combinatorial optimization problems that share structural properties with large-scale constrained inference tasks in machine learning.
